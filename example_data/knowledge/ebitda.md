# EBITDA

A profitability measure that removes the effects of capital structure (interest), tax regime, and accounting policy on long-lived assets (depreciation and amortisation).

## Formula

EBITDA = Net income + Interest + Tax + Depreciation + Amortisation

Equivalently:
EBITDA = Operating income + Depreciation + Amortisation

## Why it's used

- Cross-company comparability — strips differences in leverage and tax jurisdictions
- Approximates cash from operations for capital-light businesses
- Standard input for valuation multiples (EV/EBITDA)
- Common debt covenant base (e.g. Net Debt / EBITDA leverage ratio)

## When to use

- Comparing firms with different capital structures
- Valuing acquisitions via EV/EBITDA multiples
- Tracking operational performance year-over-year
- Credit analysis (leverage and coverage ratios)

## Pitfalls

- Not a substitute for free cash flow — ignores CapEx, working capital movements, and tax actually paid
- Capital-intensive businesses have high D&A for a reason; EBITDA flatters them
- "Adjusted EBITDA" can hide repeat one-offs and stock-based compensation
- Charlie Munger called it "bullshit earnings" for businesses that need ongoing reinvestment
- Always cross-check with FCF and CapEx-to-revenue trends
