# Porter's Five Forces

A structural framework for assessing the long-run profit potential of an industry. The premise: industry profitability is determined more by structure than by individual firm strategy.

## The five forces

1. **Threat of new entrants** — entry barriers (capital, scale, regulation, brand). Low barriers compress incumbent margins.
2. **Bargaining power of suppliers** — concentration of suppliers, switching costs, importance of input. High supplier power transfers profit upstream.
3. **Bargaining power of buyers** — concentration of buyers, price sensitivity, switching costs. High buyer power compresses prices.
4. **Threat of substitutes** — alternative products that meet the same need. Strong substitutes cap pricing power.
5. **Rivalry among existing competitors** — number of firms, growth rate, exit barriers, fixed cost structure. Intense rivalry erodes margins.

## When to use

- Entry/exit decisions
- Long-term strategic positioning
- M&A target evaluation (industry-level due diligence)
- Pricing strategy reviews

## Pitfalls

- Snapshot framework; doesn't capture dynamics or disruption well
- Treats industry boundaries as fixed; technology change blurs them
- Ignores complementors (sometimes added as a sixth force)
- Best used alongside scenario analysis, not as a standalone verdict
- Each force should be rated explicitly (low/medium/high) with evidence, not hand-waved
